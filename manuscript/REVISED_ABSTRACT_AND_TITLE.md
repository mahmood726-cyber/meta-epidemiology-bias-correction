### **Objective**

To quantify agreement between randomized controlled trial (RCT) and observational study treatment effects using advanced bias-correction methods and determine whether publication bias affects meta-epidemiological comparisons.

### **Methods**

We conducted a meta-epidemiological study comparing seven statistical approaches: standard random-effects meta-analysis, Hartung-Knapp adjustment, PET-PEESE bias correction, trim-and-fill, limit meta-analysis, and Bayesian MCMC with prior sensitivity. We analyzed 57 comparisons from the IMPACT-HTA database and published meta-epidemiological reviews. The primary outcome was the ratio of odds ratios (RoR) comparing observational to RCT effect estimates. We investigated why selection models failed and conducted comprehensive publication bias assessment.

### **Results**

Standard random-effects meta-analysis showed no significant bias (RoR = 1.020, 95% CI 0.913-1.140, p = 0.73). However, Egger's test detected strong publication bias (p < 0.001). After bias correction, all methods showed consistent underestimation: PEESE RoR = 0.906 (9% underestimation), trim-and-fill RoR = 0.870 (13%), limit meta-analysis RoR = 0.867 (13%), median = 0.887 (11%). Bayesian MCMC with weakly informative prior was inconclusive (RoR = 1.033, 95% credible interval 0.534-2.007). Selection models failed due to insufficient sample size (N=57<100 required) and high heterogeneity. Heterogeneity was extreme (I² = 91%, prediction interval 0.50-2.06), with 65% of comparisons concordant (discordance index <2) and 35% discordant. Concordant comparisons showed minimal heterogeneity (I² = 0%), while discordant comparisons were highly variable (I² = 98%).

### **Conclusions**

Standard meta-analysis masks publication bias in meta-epidemiological comparisons. Advanced bias-correction methods consistently reveal modest underestimation (9-13%) by observational studies. However, extreme heterogeneity (prediction interval spanning 50% underestimation to 106% overestimation) indicates that agreement is highly context-dependent. Selection models are inappropriate for meta-epidemiological data with N<100. Results demonstrate the importance of applying publication bias correction to meta-epidemiological evidence.

**Keywords:** Meta-epidemiology, publication bias, bias-correction methods, observational studies, randomized controlled trials, PET-PEESE, heterogeneity

## SAMPLE SIZE JUSTIFICATION (For Methods Section)

### **Sample Size**

This analysis included all available real comparisons from a comprehensive systematic search. We identified 57 comparisons from two sources: the IMPACT-HTA database (n=8) and a published meta-epidemiological review in BMC Medicine 2022 (n=49). Although larger datasets exist (Anglemyer 2014: 1,583 comparisons), individual-level comparison data were not publicly accessible despite contact attempts. Post-hoc power analysis indicated 6% power to detect 10% underestimation (RoR=0.90) and 5% power to detect 5% underestimation (RoR=0.95) with N=57. While modest, our sample size exceeds many individual meta-epidemiological reviews (typical N=20-50) and is comparable to recent high-quality studies (e.g., Nutrition BMC 2025: N=64). The sample size was adequate to assess publication bias and compare multiple analytical methods, though limited power for subgroup analyses is acknowledged as a limitation.

## CONCORDANCE DEFINITION (For Methods Section)

### **Concordance Assessment**

Concordance was defined using the discordance index (DI), calculated as |log RoR| / SE(RoR). The primary threshold was DI < 2, indicating that the difference between RCT and observational estimates was less than twice the standard error. We conducted sensitivity analyses using alternative thresholds (DI < 1.5, 2.5, and 3.0) to assess robustness to this arbitrary cutoff. Additionally, we examined concordance using an alternative definition (same direction of effect) to provide multiple perspectives on agreement.

## HETEROGENEITY EXPLORATION (For Results Section)

### **Heterogeneity Analysis**

Overall heterogeneity was extreme (I² = 91.2%, Tau² = 0.126), with a prediction interval of 0.50 to 2.06, indicating that future individual comparisons could range from 50% underestimation to 106% overestimation.

When stratified by concordance status, we observed markedly different patterns:
- **Concordant comparisons (DI < 2, n=37):** RoR = 0.967 (95% CI 0.933-1.002), **I² = 0%** (no heterogeneity)
- **Discordant comparisons (DI ≥ 2, n=20):** RoR = 1.024 (95% CI 0.745-1.407), **I² = 97.7%** (extreme heterogeneity)

Among discordant comparisons, direction was evenly split: 55% showed observational underestimation and 45% showed overestimation.

Concordance rates varied with threshold: 53% (DI < 1.5), **65% (DI < 2.0)**, 79% (DI < 2.5), and 83% (DI < 3.0). Using an alternative definition (same direction of effect), 70% were concordant.

These findings demonstrate that heterogeneity is driven almost entirely by the 35% of discordant comparisons, while concordant comparisons show remarkable homogeneity.

## LIMITATIONS SECTION (Additions)

### **Additional Limitations to Include**

**Multiple Testing:**
We tested seven different methods without correction for multiple comparisons. However, our focus was on consistency across methods rather than hypothesis testing for individual approaches. The convergence of all bias-corrected methods (range 0.867-0.906) suggests a robust finding despite multiple testing.

**Post-Hoc Method Choice:**
The designation of PEESE as the primary bias-corrected method (when bias detected) was based on methodological recommendations rather than a priori specification. This represents a limitation of our study design, though the consistency across multiple bias-correction approaches provides reassurance.

**Bayesian Prior Choice:**
Our Bayesian analysis used a weakly informative prior, which resulted in an inconclusive posterior (95% CrI 0.534-2.007) due to extreme heterogeneity. More informative priors based on external evidence would be needed for Bayesian approaches to provide useful inference in this context.

**Concordance Threshold:**
The DI < 2 threshold for concordance is statistical rather than clinical, and somewhat arbitrary. Sensitivity analyses showed concordance rates ranging from 53% to 83% depending on threshold choice (DI 1.5 to 3.0), though our primary definition (DI < 2) aligns with conventional practice.
