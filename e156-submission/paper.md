Bias-Correction Methods Reveal Underestimation by Observational Studies

Do standard random-effects meta-analyses mask systematic publication bias in meta-epidemiological comparisons of randomized and observational studies? We assembled 57 ratio-of-ratios comparisons from the IMPACT-HTA database and a BMC Medicine 2022 review, then applied seven methods including REML, Hartung-Knapp, PET-PEESE, trim-and-fill, limit meta-analysis, selection models, and Bayesian MCMC. The standard estimate showed no significant bias, but after correction all convergent methods indicated 9 to 13 percent underestimation by observational studies. The median corrected RR was 0.887, 95% CI 0.764-0.989, with extreme heterogeneity at I-squared 91.2 percent driven entirely by 35 percent discordant comparisons. Selection models failed for all three specifications due to insufficient sample size and sparse p-value intervals in the dataset. Stratification by a novel discordance index revealed that 64.9 percent of comparisons show agreement at a threshold below two. However, a limitation is that the 57-comparison sample restricts statistical power for subgroup-specific bias correction estimates.
